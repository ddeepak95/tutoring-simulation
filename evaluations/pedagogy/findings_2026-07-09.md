# Findings: Per-Dimension Pedagogical Scoring of Tutoring Conversations

## Goal

A model that reads a raw tutoring conversation and scores it 1–5 on a chosen pedagogical
dimension (target dimensions: Cognitive Engagement, Formative Assessment,
Accountability), as a step toward comparing tutoring quality across languages.

## Setup

- **Training data:** CONVOLEARN (public HuggingFace dataset) : 2,134 unique tutoring
  conversations, each labeled by human annotators on **one** of six pedagogical
  dimensions with a consensus effectiveness score (1.0–5.0, in half-points).
  Per-dimension counts: Metacognition 589, Cognitive Engagement 501, Formative
  Assessment 289, Power Dynamics 288, Accountability 278, Cultural Responsiveness 189.
- **Model:** a fine-tuned ModernBERT (a modern BERT-style language model, 395M
  parameters). The input to the model is the dimension's name and a one-paragraph
  definition, followed by the conversation; the model outputs a single score. To score a
  new conversation on several dimensions, we run it once per dimension, swapping in the
  dimension text.
- **Data split:** 70% train / 15% validation / 15% test (325 test conversations),
  stratified by topic, fixed random seed. (The original paper split by seed question;
  that field isn't in the public release : a known limitation.)
- **Reference points:** the CONVOLEARN paper's model (a fine-tuned Longformer) reports
  r = 0.736, RMSE = 0.710, MAE = 0.530. Our own replication of that model on the public
  data reaches r = 0.672, RMSE = 0.815, MAE = 0.607.

## Result 1: held-out test metrics (n = 325)

| Metric | Fine-tuned ModernBERT |
|---|---|
| Pearson r | 0.702 |
| Spearman ρ | 0.710 |
| RMSE | 0.767 |
| MAE | 0.578 |

Per-dimension Pearson r:

| Dimension | n | r |
|---|---|---|
| Accountability | 45 | 0.749 |
| Cognitive Engagement | 83 | 0.688 |
| Cultural Responsiveness | 24 | 0.178 |
| Formative Assessment | 36 | 0.816 |
| Metacognition | 87 | 0.702 |
| Power Dynamics | 50 | 0.711 |

**Observation 1.** Fine-tuning works well for predicting overall effectiveness: the
model beats our Longformer replication on every metric and approaches the paper's
published result. The three target dimensions land at r = 0.69–0.82.

**Observation 2.** Cultural Responsiveness (r ≈ 0.18) is not learnable from only 189
training examples : a data-volume problem. It is outside our target scope.

## Result 2: does the model measure the *specific* dimension it is asked about?

Correlation with held-out labels cannot answer this: each test conversation is only
labeled on one dimension, so a model that scores generic conversation quality would look
just as good. We therefore built two controlled sets of 60 simulated tutoring
conversations each, using our own simulation pipeline (the tutor is instructed to exhibit
specified pedagogy levels).

**Check A : single-dimension extremes.** For each target dimension, 10 conversations
engineered to be Very High on it and 10 Very Low (everything else neutral).

- The model separates the Very-High from the Very-Low conversations almost perfectly
  when asked about the manipulated dimension (AUC 0.96 / 0.995 / 1.0 for Accountability
  / Cognitive Engagement / Formative Assessment; 1.0 = perfect separation).
- **But** the separation is not specific: the Very-High vs. Very-Low score gap is
  essentially the same size on all six dimensions the model can be asked about, and the
  manipulated dimension never shows the largest gap.

**Check B : cross-dimension contrast (the decisive test).** 60 conversations engineered
to be **Very High on dimension A and simultaneously Very Low on dimension B** (all 6
ordered pairs of the three target dimensions × 10). A dimension-specific scorer must give
the same conversation a higher score on A than on B.

- The model ranks A above B in 62% of conversations : barely above the 50% coin-flip
  baseline, and even that overstates it: each dimension has a fixed scoring offset (e.g.,
  the model's Formative Assessment scores run slightly lower than its Cognitive
  Engagement scores on *any* conversation). After cancelling these offsets by pairing
  each cell with its mirror image (A-high/B-low vs. B-high/A-low), the genuine
  within-conversation difference the model detects is **≤ ~0.02 points on the 1–5
  scale** : effectively zero.

**Observation 3.** The model is not dimension-specific. Its per-dimension scores are one
shared "overall quality" judgment plus a small fixed offset per dimension. The most
likely cause is the structure of the training labels: every CONVOLEARN conversation is
scored on only one dimension, quality co-varies strongly across dimensions, and the model
is never shown the same conversation rated high on one dimension and low on another : so
it is never forced to learn what distinguishes the constructs.

**Observation 4 (ceiling effect).** On the Check-B conversations, the model scores
~4.9–5.1 on *every* dimension : including the one engineered to be Very Low. Compare
Check A, where Very-Low conversations dropped ~0.5 points across the board: one strongly
exhibited good practice lifts the perceived quality of the whole conversation to the top
of the scale, hiding the engineered deficit. This also reinterprets Check A's near-perfect
AUCs: the model was detecting overall polish, not the specific construct.

**Caveat.** Check B assumes the simulator faithfully produced the contrast (e.g., that an
"Accountability-high / Cognitive-Engagement-low" transcript really does exhibit low
cognitive engagement). Asking a tutor model to be very good at one practice and very poor
at another at the same time is an unusual instruction, and fidelity has not yet been
verified. Until it is, Observation 3 is strongly indicated but provisional.

## Implications and proposed next steps

1. **Verify Check B's conversations with a zero-shot LLM judge** (using our written
   dimension rubrics). This doubles as a diagnostic: if a judge *can* rank the high
   dimension above the low one where our fine-tuned model cannot, the constructs are
   expressible in text and the limitation is confirmed to be the single-dimension
   training labels; if the judge also fails, the test set needs regeneration. (This also
   covers most of the planned LLM-judge baseline.)
2. **If confirmed, two viable paths to dimension-specific scoring:**
   (a) use the LLM judge directly as the per-dimension scorer : it is multilingual for
   free, which suits the cross-language goal : keeping the fine-tuned model as an
   overall-quality anchor; and/or (b) generate multi-dimension-labeled training data with
   our own simulator (it assigns a level to *every* dimension in every conversation :
   exactly the supervision CONVOLEARN lacks) and fine-tune on that.
3. **What stands regardless:** the fine-tuned ModernBERT is a strong scorer of overall
   tutoring effectiveness (r = 0.70 on held-out data, matching or beating prior work on
   the public data), and it transfers well to our simulated conversations : a solid
   foundation and baseline for whatever provides the dimension-specific signal.
